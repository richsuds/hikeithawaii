About our Mud and Waterfall Estimates


    The Trail Conditions (Muddiness) and Waterfall Flow estimates provided by Hikeit Hawaii are based on observed rain reports throughout the state. These estimates are exclusive to Hikeit Hawaii. Accuracy of these estimates vary by location as some trails are closer to observation points than others. We are constantly monitoring and updating the estimate algorithms based on feedback from the community and our own explorations of trails to verify condition accuracy.

    All weather observations and weather forecasts are generated by the National Weather Service Honolulu and are relayed to hikeithawaii.com to consolidate the hiking weather and conditions into one convenient place.

    *Disclaimer: The conditions, estimates, observations, and forecasts provided by HikeIt Hawaii are for informational purposes only. Weather data collected from the National Weather Service could be outdated or inaccurate. Use caution and check all available resources before hiking. Why Weather and Conditions matter


    Hiking is one of the most popular activities in Hawai’i, and for good reason. The weather is ideal year round and there’s a lot of options when it comes to choosing what type of hike you’d like to do and the various scenic options you want to experience. Hawai’is scenic views, waterfalls, and plant life primarily stem from the unique and drastic contrast between mountain (Mauka) and ocean (Makai). The combination of tropical weather and volcanic terrain provide some of the best hiking experiences anywhere in the world.

    The terrain in Hawai’i has a significant influence on the local weather as well as the effects of the weather. It creates micro climates, causing clouds and rain in certain areas (typically the windward and mountain regions) and drier climates in others (Leeward regions). The high elevation on some of the mountains can create freezing climates during winter months.

    Combined, the terrain and rainfall can also lead to dangerous scenarios where hikers get lost, caught in flash floods, or even slip and fall off cliffs. Many of these incidents can be avoided by checking weather conditions before going and understanding what type of affect the weather conditions will have on trails.

    Typical weather patterns for the majority of the lower lying terrain (3,000 ft and below) consist of scattered clouds and isolated rain showers, although thunderstorms (with lightning and hail), flash floods, and droughts are relatively common during their respective seasons. Less common weather impacts are from hurricanes and tornadoes/waterspouts. The summits of Mauna Loa, Mauna Kea, and Haleakala can experience blizzards and snow during winter months.

    Hawai’is rainiest season is winter (October-April). The main source for rain annually is the trade winds (easterly winds) interacting with the terrain, but winter storms can greatly enhance overall annual rainfall.

    Rainfall and terrain have a direct affect on how slippery the trails are and whether or not flash flooding is likely. While often sunny on the Leeward side of the island, the Windward side consistently experiences rain showers and clouds. Unless hikers check on the weather each day, it can be hard to know how the previous few days of weather will impact the hike they plan to do. Many may be under the impression that the weather has been nice and sunny with safe hiking conditions, then they get to the other side of the island to hike and the trails and muddy and slippery.

    Just as checking the weather conditions will help you safely plan your hike, it can also be used to plan and arrive at a waterfall during peak flow instead of being let down by a weak trickle.

    Hikeit Hawai’i was designed with hiking safety in mind. It is here to help you plan a safe trip and to find a flowing waterfall by tracking the weather to measure estimated trail muddiness and waterfall flow. It also provides current weather and a 3-Day forecast enabling hikers to plan before they go or a few days out.

    The homepage shows general weather relevant to all Hawai’ian Islands, National Weather Service issued hazards, watches, warnings, and advisories (this is where flash flood information can be found), and a current satellite loop to show what type of weather is on its way.

    Each island has its own unique climates, terrain, and weather patterns so it is important to research the trails you plan on doing well in advance. Check specific islands forecasts and conditions by navigating to your islands before you head out to find the latest weather impacts.

    We are constantly checking posts through social media and various other sources to verify the accuracy of our information and appreciate any feedback!
